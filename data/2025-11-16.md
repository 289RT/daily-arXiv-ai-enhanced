### [1] [Prebiotic Chemistry Insights for Dragonfly: Thermodynamics of Amino Acid Synthesis in Selk Crater on Titan](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.09636)
*Ishaan Madan,Ben K. D. Pearce*

Main category: astro-ph.EP

TL;DR: 论文探讨了土卫六上撞击生成的熔池如何通过化学平衡促进氨基酸合成，揭示了NH3的关键作用及可能的替代路径，提出了可实验验证的建议。

Motivation: 研究土卫六上的预生物化学环境，为地球早期生命起源提供类比，探索NH3对氨基酸合成的关键影响。

Method: 使用Cantera平衡模型分析HCN、C2H2和NH3混合物在撞击熔池中的反应，结合实验动力学数据进行对比。

Result: NH3显著促进氨基酸合成；发现了NH3-free路径下醛氨酸的替代合成途径；化学平衡时间远短于熔池寿命，支持实验可行性。

Conclusion: 土卫六的撞击熔池是研究预生物化学的理想场所，NH3是关键因素，实验结果可通过Dragonfly任务验证。

Abstract: Saturnian moon Titan presents a compelling testbed for probing prebiotic chemistry beyond early Earth. Impact-generated melt pools provide transient aqueous habitats in an otherwise cryogenic environment. We use Cantera equilibrium models to assess whether mixtures of hydrogen cyanide (HCN), acetylene (C2H2), and ammonia (NH3) can drive amino acid synthesis in Selk-sized craters. Across twenty-one amino acids (twenty proteinogenic plus beta-alanine), NH3-free systems yield only proline, alanine, and beta-alanine, whereas adding as little as 1% NH3 (relative to H2O) renders almost the full suite accessible, with yields peaking at 2% and tapering thereafter. The NH3-free alanine result implies alternative pathways beyond classical Strecker or aminonitrile hydrolysis, suggesting acetylene, abundant on Titan but scarce on early Earth, as a plausible feedstock. We identify acrylonitrile (detected on Titan) as a thermodynamically favorable intermediate that can convert to alanine under aqueous conditions in an NH3-free pathway. For glycine and alanine production from nitrile hydrolysis, comparison with laboratory kinetics shows that our equilibrium models predict near-complete conversion, while observed rates yield only partial products over weeks. Yet estimated chemical equilibration times (years-centuries) are far shorter than melt lifetimes, supporting plausibility of equilibrium in situ. These predictions are directly testable with Dragonfly mass spectrometer (DraMS), for which we recommend pre-flight standards to test proline, alanine, beta-alanine, cysteine, and methionine. The first three offer the best chances for amino acid detection regardless of ammonia availability; the latter two offer diagnostic tools for determining the presence of reactive sulfur in post-impact Titan ponds.
